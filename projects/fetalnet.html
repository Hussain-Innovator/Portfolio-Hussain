FetalNet — Fine-Grained Classification
of Fetal Ultrasound Planes

A lightweight deep learning system that automatically identifies six fetal anatomical planes in prenatal ultrasound scans — achieving 93.01% accuracy with full explainable AI support so clinicians can see exactly why the model made each call.

Supervised by Mr. Fahad Najeeb Team: Hussain · Javed Iqbal · Ali Abbas Dataset: FETAL_PLANES_DB · 12,400 images Framework: PyTorch · Streamlit

Project overview

Prenatal ultrasound is one of the most widely used diagnostic tools in modern obstetrics — yet accurate reading of the scans still depends heavily on the experience of the sonographer. A tired clinician, a noisy image, or a sub-optimal fetal position can introduce inconsistency that affects diagnosis. FetalNet was built to act as a second opinion: a fast, consistent AI classifier that runs on a standard CPU and tells you which anatomical plane you are looking at, along with a visual explanation of its reasoning.

"Manual ultrasound interpretation is time-intensive and subject to observer variability. Our goal was to make AI-assisted classification accessible, interpretable, and lightweight enough to run in resource-constrained clinical environments."

The model combines a frozen MobileNetV2 backbone (pre-trained on ImageNet) with a custom convolutional head and a Squeeze-and-Excitation attention block — letting the network dynamically decide which feature channels matter most for each image. Four XAI techniques are wired directly into the Streamlit interface so radiologists get a heatmap alongside every prediction.

Dataset — FETAL_PLANES_DB

All images come from BCNatal, a maternal-fetal medicine research centre in Barcelona, Spain (Hospital Clínic + Hospital Sant Joan de Déu). Scans were captured between October 2018 and April 2019 using six different ultrasound machines — three Voluson E6, one Voluson S10, one Voluson S8, and one Aloka — ensuring the dataset reflects real-world machine variability. Images were stored as raw DICOM, anonymised, and converted to uncompressed PNG.

Explainable AI — four interpretability techniques

High accuracy alone is not enough for clinical adoption. FetalNet integrates four complementary XAI techniques so a radiologist can always ask "why did the model predict this?" and get a visual answer rather than a black-box confidence score.

🔥
Grad-CAM
Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping. Produces a coarse heatmap over the image highlighting the broad spatial regions the network focused on — e.g. the fetal skull boundary for brain scans. Fast and reliable for confirming the model looked at the right anatomy.
🎯
Guided Backpropagation
Combines gradient flow with ReLU gating to produce sharp, pixel-level edge maps. Reveals fine-grained texture and boundary features that drive the decision — useful for catching cases where the model focuses on image artefacts rather than anatomy.
📊
Integrated Gradients
Attributes prediction to each pixel by accumulating gradients along a path from a baseline (black image) to the input. Produces stable, theoretically grounded saliency maps that satisfy completeness and sensitivity axioms — preferred for rigorous model auditing.
🕵️
Occlusion Sensitivity
Systematically masks patches of the image and measures how much the prediction changes. Identifies critical regions by perturbation — slow but effective for edge cases and for finding blind spots the gradient methods may miss.

Architecture pipeline

Input image
Grayscale ultrasound scan (PNG / JPG)
Stage 0
Raw ultrasound scan
The system accepts a fetal ultrasound image captured during a routine prenatal scan. Images originate from the public FETAL_PLANES_DB dataset — 12,400 grayscale scans collected across six ultrasound machines at BCNatal, Spain. Six anatomical categories are covered: fetal brain, abdomen, femur, thorax, maternal cervix, and other.
Preprocessing
Grayscale → RGB · 224×224 · normalise mean/std = 0.5
Stage 1 — image preparation
Standardising the input
Grayscale images are converted to 3-channel RGB (required by MobileNetV2's pre-trained weights), resized to 224×224 pixels, and normalised with mean = [0.5, 0.5, 0.5] and std = [0.5, 0.5, 0.5]. This standardises pixel intensity across different scan machines, operators, and imaging conditions, making training more stable.
MobileNetV2 backbone
18 inverted residual blocks · frozen weights · output: 1,280 channels
Stage 2 — transfer learning
Pre-trained feature extractor
MobileNetV2 is a lightweight CNN pre-trained on ImageNet's 1.2 million images. Its 18 inverted residual blocks use depthwise-separable convolutions to extract rich visual features at low computational cost. All backbone weights are frozen during Phase 1 — the network's general knowledge is preserved and only the new custom head is trained. This avoids overfitting on the relatively small medical dataset. Output: a 1,280-channel feature map.
Conv block 1
Conv2d(1280→256, 3×3) · BatchNorm · ReLU · MaxPool(2)
Stage 3 — spatial refinement
First convolutional refinement layer
A 3×3 convolution reduces channel depth from 1,280 to 256, followed by Batch Normalisation (stabilises and accelerates training) and ReLU activation (introduces non-linearity). MaxPool(2) halves spatial resolution, compressing information while retaining the most important activations. This layer adapts generic ImageNet features toward ultrasound-specific patterns such as tissue boundaries and echo textures.
SE block — squeeze-and-excitation
AvgPool → FC(256→32) → ReLU → FC(32→256) → Sigmoid → channel scale
Stage 4 — attention (key innovation)
Channel-wise attention mechanism
The SE Block is the architectural highlight of FetalNet. It asks: "which of the 256 feature channels matter most for this particular image?" It squeezes spatial dimensions to a single value per channel via global average pooling, passes them through two FC layers with a bottleneck (reduction = 8, so 256→32→256), applies Sigmoid to produce per-channel weights in [0,1], and multiplies those weights back onto the feature map — dynamically amplifying informative channels (e.g. skull boundary for brain, diaphragm for thorax) and suppressing noise. The ablation study confirmed that removing the SE Block dropped accuracy from 93.01% to 92.00%.
Conv block 2
Conv2d(256→128, 3×3) · BatchNorm · ReLU · AdaptiveAvgPool(1×1)
Stage 5 — compression
Second convolutional refinement layer
A second 3×3 convolution further compresses channels from 256 to 128. Adaptive Average Pooling then collapses the spatial dimensions entirely to a 1×1 grid, producing a compact 128-dimensional feature vector. This removes spatial position from the representation — leaving only "what anatomical pattern is present", not "where in the image it appears". Removing this layer in the ablation study reduced accuracy to 92.64%.
Classifier head
Flatten → FC(128→128) → ReLU → Dropout(0.5) → FC(128→6)
Stage 6 — decision-making layers
Fully connected classification head
The 128-d vector is passed through a fully connected layer FC(128→128) with ReLU, then Dropout at rate 0.5. Dropout randomly deactivates 50% of neurons during training, forcing the network to learn redundant representations and acting as strong regularisation to prevent memorising training examples. The final layer FC(128→6) maps to logits for the six anatomical classes.
Output — 6 classes + Softmax
Brain · Abdomen · Femur · Thorax · Cervix · Other
Stage 7 — prediction output
Final classification & confidence score
The 6 raw logits are converted to probabilities via Softmax. The class with the highest probability is returned to the Streamlit web application as the predicted anatomical plane, alongside a confidence score and XAI visualisations (Grad-CAM, Guided Backpropagation, Integrated Gradients, Occlusion Sensitivity). Best recall: Maternal Cervix (100%). Lowest recall: Fetal Abdomen (79%), reflecting class imbalance in the training data.

Two-phase training strategy

Phase 1 — baseline training (10 epochs)
MobileNetV2 backbone frozen. Only the custom convolutional head and classifier are trained. Adam optimiser, learning rate = 1e-4, batch size = 32, Cross-Entropy loss. Light augmentation (horizontal flip, random rotation). Best validation accuracy: 92.69%. Final test accuracy: 91.77%.
Phase 2 — fine-tuning (10 more epochs)
Upper layers of MobileNetV2 gradually unfrozen. Entire network trained end-to-end. Learning rate reduced to 1e-5 to avoid overwriting pre-trained features. Dropout = 0.5 for regularisation. Best validation accuracy: 93.34%. Final test accuracy: 93.01%. Weighted F1: 0.93.
